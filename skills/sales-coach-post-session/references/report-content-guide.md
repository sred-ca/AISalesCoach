# Report Content Guide

Tone and content rules for generating Evan's Coaching Report and the Manager Summary.
Read this when you're about to write the narrative sections of any coaching report.

## Evan's Report — Tone Rules

**Voice: John talking to Evan after a coaching call.**

Evan knows John. He just had a conversation with him. The written report should feel like a follow-up from a real person, not a corporate HR document. It's direct, specific, and warm in the way that two people who respect each other can be direct.

**DO:**
- Reference specific moments from the actual call: "When Logan came in and took over the discovery portion, you went quiet. That's a pattern worth naming."
- Quote Evan's own words back to him (especially his commitments)
- Celebrate wins as earned, not as participation trophies: "You got that Think CNC follow-up out in under 90 minutes. That's not chance — that's discipline."
- Name one thing and go deep on it
- End with genuine encouragement, not hollow cheerleading

**DON'T:**
- Generic advice: "Keep asking discovery questions" — useless without context
- Corporate language: "leveraging synergies," "moving the needle"
- Disappointed parent tone: "I'm concerned that Evan continues to..."
- Fake enthusiasm: "AMAZING week!" when it wasn't amazing
- More than one coaching focus. Pick one. The second thing can wait.

**Win callouts — write them like this:**
- ✅ "Think CNC — you didn't pitch. You supported Logan's discovery and let the client drive. That's the kind of maturity that turns meetings into relationships."
- ✅ "BWS Solutions — you got a yes on the first call. That doesn't happen without preparation."
- ❌ "Great job following up on Think CNC!" — too vague, too cheerful

**Coaching focus section — write it like this:**
- Pick ONE thing. Give 2-3 paragraphs.
- Para 1: What the pattern is (with a specific example from the week)
- Para 2: Why it matters in SR&ED sales specifically
- Para 3: What to try this week (a concrete behavior, not a platitude)

**Example coaching focus section:**

> *Talk ratio.* In the Think CNC call you were at 10% — which is actually your highest when Jude or Logan is present. The Spearhead call back in February? 3.9%, zero questions. The difference is clear: when a strong voice is in the room, you defer. That's not a confidence issue — it's a habit.
>
> In SR&ED sales specifically, your talk ratio matters at the discovery stage because the questions you ask are what build the technical case. When Logan does the discovery, the information goes to Logan. Your job is to understand their R&D activities well enough to write the claim. That means you need to be in the conversation, not observing it.
>
> This week: in every meeting, commit to asking at least three questions before anyone else on your team speaks. They can be simple. "What's your timeline on this project?" works. The goal is to build the habit of claiming your space early.

## Manager Summary — Tone Rules

**Voice: John debriefing Jude after a session.**

Jude is a founder. He's busy. He cares about Evan. He wants signal, not a transcript. Write this like you're giving him a 2-minute verbal update in the hallway.

**DO:**
- Lead with the most important thing first
- Be direct about Evan's energy and receptivity: "He came in a bit flat, probably end-of-week energy. Picked up significantly when we got into the Think CNC specifics."
- Name what Jude can do that coaching can't: "The VassuTech deal is 40 days past its close date. Evan's stuck — it might take a Jude call to get clarity on whether this is real."
- Be honest about SDT: "He's starting to own the pipeline narrative a bit more — moved from 'I should' to 'I want to' on the discovery front. Watch this over the next 4-6 sessions."

**DON'T:**
- Summarize the transcript ("First John asked Evan about...")
- Include Evan's personal goals (strictly Evan + Coach only)
- Make it longer than 4-5 short sections
- Use soft language when a deal is dead: "The MSR Solutions deal has been silent for 40 days. It's probably dead — Evan should close it out or Jude should make one escalation call."

**"How the Session Went" section — examples:**

Good:
> Evan came in self-aware this week — he opened by flagging the VassuTech stall before I brought it up. That's a shift from the first session where he was more reactive. He pushed back once on the InMail critique ("InMails are what the Prospecting Agent does, not me") — fair point, I adjusted. Committed to the personal touch-up cadence without resistance.

Bad:
> The session was productive. Evan engaged well with the coaching topics and showed openness to feedback. He committed to several action items.

**"Manager Insight" section — only include things Jude can actually act on:**

1. **Deal support needed:** "FanLINC/Sparcks — Evan's been at Follow-Up for 3 weeks. Jude visibility might accelerate."
2. **Resource gaps:** "Evan is manually tracking all re-engagement — a structured CRM task reminder sequence in HubSpot would help."
3. **Team dynamics:** "If Logan is in more Technical Discovery calls, make sure Evan understands his role is discovery lead, not support."
4. **Systemic:** "The InMail volume is burning credits at 5.8% return. Worth reviewing the HeyReach strategy with Evan directly."

## What NOT to Include in Evan's Report

- Company-wide QuickBooks revenue (Jude only)
- Evan's personal goals (by name or quotation)
- SDT ladder labels in clinical terms ("you're at Decision Ladder Rung 2") — instead: "you're starting to own the strategy more"
- Anything that makes him feel surveilled rather than coached
- Jude's personal thoughts or reactions from the Manager Summary

## Commitment Section — Format

Pull the exact words Evan used in the call. Don't paraphrase.

If Evan said: *"Okay yeah, I'll send personal re-engagement emails to VassuTech, TeckPath, and MSR by Thursday. Something short, just checking in."*

Write exactly that. Not: "Evan committed to sending re-engagement emails to three stalled deals."

Then add: **What John will ask about next Monday:**
- Did the three emails go out?
- What response, if any?
- Was Thursday the right deadline, or did something get in the way?
