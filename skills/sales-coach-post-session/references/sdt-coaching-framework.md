# Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Coaching Framework

This framework guides how the coaching system evaluates Evan's psychological progression. SDT provides the theoretical backbone for the coaching approach — the goal is to move Evan from external motivation (compliance) toward internalized, self-directed performance.

## Core SDT Needs

Every coaching interaction should be evaluated against three innate psychological needs:

**Autonomy** — Does Evan feel ownership over his sales process, or is he following instructions?
- Low autonomy signals: asking permission before acting, waiting for Jude/Logan to lead, deferring on discovery questions
- High autonomy signals: initiating outreach without prompting, choosing coaching focus areas, self-correcting after a bad call

**Competence** — Does Evan believe he can succeed, and is that belief grounded in evidence?
- Low competence signals: avoiding difficult prospects, sticking to safe/scripted approaches, deflecting when challenged
- High competence signals: adapting approach mid-call, accurately self-assessing performance, seeking harder challenges

**Relatedness** — Does Evan feel connected to the team and the coaching process?
- Low relatedness signals: disengagement in calls, minimal sharing, treating coaching as a checkbox
- High relatedness signals: bringing up challenges unprompted, asking for feedback, referencing team wins

## The Motivation Ladder

Use this ladder to track where Evan's motivation sits week to week. Movement up the ladder is the primary long-term coaching objective.

| Level | Label | Description | Signals |
|-------|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Compliance | Doing it because told to | "Jude wants me to..." / only acts when prompted / no initiative |
| 2 | Ego / Should | Doing it to avoid looking bad or because "I should" | Competitive framing / shame-driven effort / inconsistent follow-through |
| 3 | Goal / Want | Doing it because of a clear personal goal | "I want to hit X" / self-set targets / effort tied to outcomes |
| 4 | Value / Identity | Doing it because "this is who I am" | "I'm the kind of person who..." / intrinsic drive / effort regardless of outcomes |

**Coaching implications:**
- Level 1→2: Provide structure and accountability. Don't push autonomy yet.
- Level 2→3: Help Evan connect activity to his own goals, not Jude's expectations.
- Level 3→4: Shift from outcome focus to process identity. Celebrate effort patterns, not just wins.

## The Decision Ladder

Tracks Evan's autonomy in decision-making over time. This ladder is about what Evan does between coaching sessions.

| Level | Label | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Told | Evan is told what to do and does it |
| 2 | Reports back | Evan acts, then checks if it was right |
| 3 | Recommends | Evan proposes a course of action and asks for approval |
| 4 | Decides + Informs | Evan makes the call and lets the team know |
| 5 | Full autonomy | Evan owns the decision end-to-end, no check-in needed |

**Coaching implications:**
- Levels 1-2: Coach should ask before telling. "What do you think you should do?" before offering advice.
- Level 3: Coach validates Evan's recommendations, reinforces good judgment.
- Levels 4-5: Coach shifts to pattern-level feedback, not tactical advice.

## How to Use This Framework in Reports

**In Evan's Coaching Report (Page 7: A Note on Growth):**
- Reference the motivation ladder in plain language, not clinical terms
- Example: "This week you didn't wait for anyone to tell you to re-engage those stalled deals — that's the shift from 'I should' to 'I want to.'"
- Never say "You're at Level 2 on the Motivation Ladder" — that's surveillance language

**In Jude's Manager Summary (Page 5: SDT Progression Note):**
- Be direct about ladder positions using the labels
- Note movement: "Evan moved from Compliance to Goal-directed on follow-up emails this week"
- Flag concerns: "Coaching focus is still externally driven — Evan frames effort as 'what Jude expects' rather than his own standard"
- Flag dependency risk: "If Evan only performs when the coaching system is watching, we're building compliance, not competence"

## Grit/Grace Balance

Every coaching session should be evaluated for Grit/Grace balance:

**Grit** = directness, accountability, honest feedback about gaps
**Grace** = empathy, encouragement, recognition of effort and difficulty

The ideal session has both. Warning signs:
- All grit, no grace → Evan shuts down, gets defensive, disengages
- All grace, no grit → Evan feels good but doesn't grow, no behavior change
- The right balance → Evan leaves the call energized AND with a clear action item

## Session Analysis Checklist

When analyzing a coaching transcript, check:

1. Did the coach ask before telling? (Autonomy support)
2. Did the coach reference specific evidence, not general impressions? (Competence building)
3. Did Evan bring anything up unprompted? (Relatedness/engagement signal)
4. Was ONE coaching focus maintained? (Cognitive load management)
5. Did Evan commit in his own words? (Internalization check — parroting vs. owning)
6. Was the commitment specific and time-bound? (Accountability)
7. Did the session end with energy or deflation? (Grit/Grace calibration)
